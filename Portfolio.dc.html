Adaptive Beamforming with Deep Learning for Ultrasound Image Reconstruction

DAS beamforming is fast but low-resolution; MVDR is sharp but too costly for real time. I built a lightweight MLP that learns a pixel-wise correction mapping DAS toward MVDR quality — using interpretable RF features. Trained on PICMUS phantom and in-vivo carotid data, it delivers near-MVDR resolution and sidelobe suppression at a fraction of the compute.

Python · Deep Learning · MLP · PICMUS · MVDR / DAS
01 · In Depth
The Problem

MVDR delivers excellent resolution and sidelobe suppression, but per-pixel covariance estimation and matrix inversion make it impractical in real time. DAS is fast but suffers limited resolution and clutter.

My Approach

Extract 5 interpretable features per pixel — log-DAS intensity, coherence factor, 5×5 local mean & std, normalized depth — and train a 3-layer MLP (256 neurons, ReLU) to predict a log-domain correction toward MVDR.

Data & Training

PICMUS phantom + in-vivo carotid RF data, merged to ~84k training / 15k validation pixels. Adam optimizer, batch 2048, 20 epochs — with stable, closely-tracked train/val loss and no overfitting.

Limitations & Next Steps
Limitations

Because the model works pixel-wise, it can't fully capture the spatial covariance MVDR relies on — so in noisy in-vivo scans, sharper resolution comes with a modest contrast trade-off (carotid CNR 0.61 vs. 0.73 for DAS). Performance is also sensitive to probe, frequency, and noise differences across datasets.

Next Steps

Move from pixel-wise features to RF-domain learning — UNet or beamformer-transformer architectures and covariance-aware networks — and train across more diverse, multi-center data. Deploy on GPU or embedded hardware (TensorRT) for real-time, point-of-care adaptive beamforming.

Simulating a 2D (8×8) Ultrasound Transducer in Verasonics

A capstone building the ultrasound imaging chain from scratch in Verasonics simulation: modelling a 64-element (8×8) 2D matrix array, transmitting steered plane waves, capturing RF data, and reconstructing volumetric images — then studying how pitch, frequency, and aperture govern image quality.

MATLAB · Verasonics · Plane-Wave Imaging · 3D Reconstruction
02 · In Depth
Goal

Build and understand a full 2D matrix-array imaging pipeline in Verasonics (simulation-only) — geometry, transmit, receive, and reconstruction — as the foundation for beamforming research.

Setup

8×8 array (64 elements), 11 MHz center frequency, 300 µm pitch, cone-shaped imaging region (20° FOV) in homogeneous media with scattered point targets along depth.

Transmit

Plane-wave transmits — a single unsteered flash, and coherent compounding across five steering angles (−20°, −10°, 0°, +10°, +20°) — reconstructed on the XY / XZ / YZ planes.

Study — Pitch, Frequency & Aperture

A follow-up study asking a deceptively simple question: if higher frequency needs a smaller pitch to satisfy spatial sampling, does shrinking the pitch actually give sharper images? The answer turned out to be counterintuitive.

Background Theory
Pitch & spatial sampling
p ≤ λ / 2

To avoid spatial aliasing and grating lobes, element pitch must stay below half a wavelength. Higher center frequency shortens λ — and therefore demands a smaller pitch.

Resolution & aperture
D = N × p

Lateral resolution depends on wavelength and aperture size. Pitch controls aliasing; aperture size (elements × pitch) controls beamwidth — and thus resolution.

The counterintuitive finding

Reducing the pitch to satisfy the Nyquist criterion did not improve image quality — point targets merged and images grew blurrier. For a fixed number of elements, a smaller pitch means a smaller physical aperture, and a smaller aperture widens the beam.

Simulation Design — Three Cases
01
Fixed pitch, varying frequency
pitch 0.30 mm · fc 3 / 7 / 11 MHz
Large aperture preserved → resolution improves.
02
Scaled pitch with frequency
pitch → 0.07 mm at 11 MHz
Aperture shrinks → beam broadening & point merging.
03
Scaled array size
16×16 (256 el.) · pitch 0.30 mm
Aperture increases → narrower beam, better resolution.

The takeaway: Reducing element pitch on a fixed 64-element array does not improve quality as expected. Since aperture size is governed by D=Nxp, smaller pitch reduces total aperture, increase bandwidth, and ultimately  degrades lateral resolution, causing point target to merged and blurry. This finding showed that maintaining sufficient aperture size is more critical than satisfying Nyquist alone.

MACHINE LEARNING

Cancer Detection via SERS Raman Spectroscopy

A machine-learning pipeline comparing Random Forest, SVM, and CNN for classifying cancer vs. normal cells from surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy (SERS) data. I built a preprocessing pipeline with RamanSPy — baseline correction, denoising, normalization — then trained and evaluated three models, inspired by Zhang et al.'s 2024 breast-cancer Raman study. A group-aware evaluation exposed data leakage that had inflated CNN accuracy by nearly 20 points, revealing SVM as the more reliable model once evaluation was corrected.

Python · scikit-learn · PyTorch · RamanSPy · Data Leakage Analysis
View on GitHub ↗
03 · In Depth
Goal

Classify cancer vs. normal cells from SERS Raman spectra using classical ML and deep learning — and rigorously validate that reported performance reflects genuine generalization rather than data leakage.

Data & Preprocessing

1,240 SERS spectra across melanoma cell lines and normal fibroblasts/melanocytes — gold-nanourchin substrates, 3 surface functionalizations. Preprocessed with RamanSPy: ASLS baseline correction, light Savitzky–Golay smoothing, min–max normalization.

Key Finding

A naive random train/test split showed 95.6% CNN accuracy, but grouping by source sample to prevent leakage dropped it to 76.2% — while SVM held steady at 92.4%, proving the more trustworthy model for this dataset size.
